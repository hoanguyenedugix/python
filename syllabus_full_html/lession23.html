Lesson Outcomes

Students can listen and identify key details about Kim and the animals he sees by choosing correct answers (e.g., matching sentence halves).

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Listening) Play short sections of the video. Ask: “What did Kim see?” Students match animal pictures to sentence halves.

Stretch Strategies:

  • "Wildlife Poster Campaign" – Students create a mini-poster encouraging people to protect a wild animal of their choice. They must include 1–2 sentences (e.g., “This is a fox. It lives in the forest. We must protect it.”).

Students can say how their lives are similar to or different from the characters in the video.

Real world learning

Support Strategies:

  • (Social Studies) Draw a chart: “My life / Kim’s life.” Students suggest what’s the same and different using animals and activities.

Stretch Strategies:

  • "Wildlife Poster Campaign" – Students create a mini-poster encouraging people to protect a wild animal of their choice. They must include 1–2 sentences (e.g., “This is a fox. It lives in the forest. We must protect it.”).

Students can discuss why it’s important to learn about wild animals and how we can protect them.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Plural forms of it is and there is:

  • Plural forms of these structures do not exist in Vietnamese. It's difficult for kids to remember to produce them, particularly because the plural element comes before the noun phrase. There are two houses
