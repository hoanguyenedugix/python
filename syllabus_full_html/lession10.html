Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six foods (pizza, fish, chicken, steak, soup, juice) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Display food flashcards or use visuals from SB. Say the word. Students repeat and point. Practise identifying from a group of 4–6 images.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Menu Roleplay Upgrade: After initial roleplay, students create their own “menus” on paper with pictures and prices. They take turns being the waiter and customer, expanding dialogue to include “How much is it?” or “I want two juices.”

Students can ask and answer simple questions about foods using "What do you want?" / "What does he/she want?" and respond with "I want..." / "He/She wants...".

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Ask: “What do you want?” Model: “I want soup.” Then ask: “What does he want?” → “He wants chicken.” Repeat with multiple options.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Menu Roleplay Upgrade: After initial roleplay, students create their own “menus” on paper with pictures and prices. They take turns being the waiter and customer, expanding dialogue to include “How much is it?” or “I want two juices.”

Students can ask and answer questions using the target structure “What do you want?” / “What does he/she want?” / “I want ___.” / “He/She wants ___.” to talk about foods.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) On board: “What do you want?” / “I want ___.” + “What does she want?” / “She wants ___.” Students practise filling gaps orally with food words.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Menu Roleplay Upgrade: After initial roleplay, students create their own “menus” on paper with pictures and prices. They take turns being the waiter and customer, expanding dialogue to include “How much is it?” or “I want two juices.”

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Silent letters:

  • Systematic kinds of silent letters, such as 'magic e' in late or mote, or silent gh in thought and bought, are entirely alien to Vietnamese. They are often acquired as sight words first; the teacher can explain at higher levels.

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
