Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six more animals (skunk, cheetah, snake, owl, peacock, fox) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show flashcards or SB visuals for owl, fox, etc. Say each word. Students listen and point or label pictures.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Animal Experts" Mini-Presentation – Students pick a zoo animal, research (or imagine) 2–3 facts, and present them using the target structure (e.g., “That’s a snake. It’s long. It eats mice.”).

Students can ask and answer simple questions about animals using "What's that?" / "What are those?" and respond with "It's a..." / "They're...".

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Point to a distant picture: “What’s that?” Students say: “It’s a skunk.” Repeat for “What are those?” using plural animals in the background.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Animal Experts" Mini-Presentation – Students pick a zoo animal, research (or imagine) 2–3 facts, and present them using the target structure (e.g., “That’s a snake. It’s long. It eats mice.”).

Students can use the target structure “What’s that?” / “What are those?” / “It’s a ___.” / “They’re ___.” to talk about animals.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) On the board: “What’s that?” – “It’s a ___.” / “What are those?” – “They’re ___.” Students practise using realia or pointing to SB pictures.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Animal Experts" Mini-Presentation – Students pick a zoo animal, research (or imagine) 2–3 facts, and present them using the target structure (e.g., “That’s a snake. It’s long. It eats mice.”).

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Plural forms of it is and there is:

  • Plural forms of these structures do not exist in Vietnamese. It's difficult for kids to remember to produce them, particularly because the plural element comes before the noun phrase. There are two houses
