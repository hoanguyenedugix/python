Lesson Outcomes

Students can correctly use the present continuous tense in positive and question forms.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write 3 scrambled sentences: “drawing / she’s,” “I’m / writing,” “we / dancing / are.” Students unscramble, read, and act them out.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Change the Scene: Show a new picture (e.g., a beach or zoo). Students describe what people might be doing using the present continuous: “The boy is eating. The girl is running.”

Students can recognise and identify the vowel sounds in “o_e” and “u_e” words.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Phonics) Say o_e and u_e words aloud. Students show 1 finger for o_e and 2 fingers for u_e, then sort words into two lists.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Change the Scene: Show a new picture (e.g., a beach or zoo). Students describe what people might be doing using the present continuous: “The boy is eating. The girl is running.”

Students can write sentences about people in a picture using the present continuous tense (e.g., “She’s drawing.”).

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Present continuous form:

  • The -ing is often forgotten. Vietnamese does not have different forms of the same verb. I like eat rice.

2. Silent letters:

  • Systematic kinds of silent letters, such as 'magic e' in late or mote, or silent gh in thought and bought, are entirely alien to Vietnamese. They are often acquired as sight words first; the teacher can explain at higher levels.

3. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

5. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.
