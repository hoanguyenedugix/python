Lesson Outcomes

Students can correctly form Yes/No questions using the verb “to play”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Display Yes/No Q&As: “Does he play the cello?” – “Yes, he does.” Students respond using mini whiteboards or thumbs up/down.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Grammar Race
  • In teams, students race to complete question–answer chains on the board (e.g., “Does she play the flute?” → “Yes, she does.” → “Does he play the cello?”). Each correct pair adds to the chain, encouraging quick recall and correct form.

Students can recognise and identify the long vowel sounds “a_e” and “i_e”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Phonics) Say a_e, i_e words aloud. Students listen and sort into two columns. Then they trace and write each word clearly.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Grammar Race
  • In teams, students race to complete question–answer chains on the board (e.g., “Does she play the flute?” → “Yes, she does.” → “Does he play the cello?”). Each correct pair adds to the chain, encouraging quick recall and correct form.

Students can recognise correct answers to Yes/No questions and complete sentence frames (e.g., “___ he play tennis?”) using the correct auxiliary verbs.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Writing) Sentence frame: “___ he play tennis?” / “Yes, he ___.” Students complete and then practise Q&A in pairs.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Question formation with do-support:

  • Bạn có thích ăn bánh mì không? >> you like eat bread? [do you like to eat bánh mì?]

2. Negatives with do-support:

  • Con không thích >> I no/not like [I don't like]

3. Sport and play:

  • Playground words tend to be long and phonetically difficult (slide, see-saw, swings, climbing frame)
  • Some sports not well known in VN (baseball, cricket, rugby, paragliding, water skiing)

4. Hobbies and activities:

  • A wide variety of syllabus hobbies may be less popular or available to some Vietnamese students (practising the flute; seeing famous paintings), although many overlap

5. Unstressed vowels (schwa):

  • Unstressed vowels do not really exist in Vietnamese, so these need extra practice. Make sure the final vowel of computer is a light schwa, not a heavy com-pu-TER. Same with the final vowel of banana. Or the second syllable of elephant. And so on.

6. Inversion in questions:

  • Bạn muốn ăn gì? >> you want to eat what? [what do you want to eat?]
  • This is extremely difficult to master, especially combined with do-support.
