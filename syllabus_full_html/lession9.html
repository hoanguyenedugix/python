Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six classroom items (marker pen, stapler, paintbrush, glue stick, colouring pencil, pencil sharpener) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show flashcards or realia (marker pen, glue stick). Say each item. Students match it to pictures in SB or label from memory.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Memory Chain Game: In a circle, students say what they and previous students have got. E.g., “I’ve got a pencil sharpener and she’s got a stapler.” Continue around the circle, adding one item each time. Supports recall, sentence building, and listening.

Students can ask and answer simple questions about classroom items using "What have you got?" / "What has he/she got?" and respond with "I've got a..." / "He's/She's got a...".

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Ask: “What have you got?” Show item. Student responds: “I’ve got a stapler.” Practise Q&A in pairs with classroom item cards.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Memory Chain Game: In a circle, students say what they and previous students have got. E.g., “I’ve got a pencil sharpener and she’s got a stapler.” Continue around the circle, adding one item each time. Supports recall, sentence building, and listening.

Students can ask and answer questions using the target structure “What have you got?” / “What has he/she got?” / “I've got a ___.” / “He's/She's got a ___.” to talk about classroom items.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write 2 columns on the board: “What have you got?” / “What has he/she got?” Students practise both forms with peer/teacher modeling.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Memory Chain Game: In a circle, students say what they and previous students have got. E.g., “I’ve got a pencil sharpener and she’s got a stapler.” Continue around the circle, adding one item each time. Supports recall, sentence building, and listening.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Silent letters:

  • Systematic kinds of silent letters, such as 'magic e' in late or mote, or silent gh in thought and bought, are entirely alien to Vietnamese. They are often acquired as sight words first; the teacher can explain at higher levels.

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
