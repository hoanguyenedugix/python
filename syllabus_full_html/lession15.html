Lesson Outcomes

Students can listen and identify key details about charity work in England by choosing correct answers (e.g., matching sentence halves).

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Listening) Play short video segments. Ask: “Who are they helping?” “What did they do?” Students match sentence halves or circle answers.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Real-Life Helpers Roleplay: In small groups, students choose a way to help others (e.g., “I help my grandma”, “He gives food”) and create a short roleplay or poster about it. They then share with the class using full sentences.

Students can say how their life is similar to or different from the characters in the video.

Real world learning

Support Strategies:

  • (Social Studies) Ask: “Have you helped someone?” “What did you do?” Students draw or describe their own experience, then compare with the video.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Real-Life Helpers Roleplay: In small groups, students choose a way to help others (e.g., “I help my grandma”, “He gives food”) and create a short roleplay or poster about it. They then share with the class using full sentences.

Students can discuss why helping others is important and give examples of how they show empathy.

Future Skills

Support Strategies:

  • (4Cs – Empathy) In groups, students brainstorm simple ways to help others. Share ideas like “help carry bags,” “visit grandma,” using drawings or actions.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Real-Life Helpers Roleplay: In small groups, students choose a way to help others (e.g., “I help my grandma”, “He gives food”) and create a short roleplay or poster about it. They then share with the class using full sentences.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Silent letters:

  • Systematic kinds of silent letters, such as 'magic e' in late or mote, or silent gh in thought and bought, are entirely alien to Vietnamese. They are often acquired as sight words first; the teacher can explain at higher levels.

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
