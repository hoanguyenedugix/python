Lesson Outcomes

Students can listen and identify key details about Nina and the sports she plays by choosing correct answers (e.g., tick the box).

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Listening) Play video and pause after each clip. Ask: “What sport did Nina do?” Students tick correct answers in the SB.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Compare & Contrast Poster Talk
  • After watching the video and completing the test, students create a simple Venn diagram or poster comparing their hobbies with the video character’s. They must present 2–3 spoken sentences explaining similarities/differences using full sentences: “I play basketball, but Nina plays tennis.”

Students can say how their hobbies are similar to or different from the characters in the video.

Real world learning

Support Strategies:

  • (Social Studies) Ask: “What sport do you play?” “What sport does Nina play?” Students compare using Venn diagram or SB visuals.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Compare & Contrast Poster Talk
  • After watching the video and completing the test, students create a simple Venn diagram or poster comparing their hobbies with the video character’s. They must present 2–3 spoken sentences explaining similarities/differences using full sentences: “I play basketball, but Nina plays tennis.”

Students can analyse a poster and then use creativity and prior knowledge to design a poster of their own.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Question formation with do-support:

  • Bạn có thích ăn bánh mì không? >> you like eat bread? [do you like to eat bánh mì?]

2. Negatives with do-support:

  • Con không thích >> I no/not like [I don't like]

3. Sport and play:

  • Playground words tend to be long and phonetically difficult (slide, see-saw, swings, climbing frame)
  • Some sports not well known in VN (baseball, cricket, rugby, paragliding, water skiing)

4. Hobbies and activities:

  • A wide variety of syllabus hobbies may be less popular or available to some Vietnamese students (practising the flute; seeing famous paintings), although many overlap

5. Unstressed vowels (schwa):

  • Unstressed vowels do not really exist in Vietnamese, so these need extra practice. Make sure the final vowel of computer is a light schwa, not a heavy com-pu-TER. Same with the final vowel of banana. Or the second syllable of elephant. And so on.

6. Inversion in questions:

  • Bạn muốn ăn gì? >> you want to eat what? [what do you want to eat?]
  • This is extremely difficult to master, especially combined with do-support.
