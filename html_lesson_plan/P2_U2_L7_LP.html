Activity A: Look and learn.
15
None
Stage aims
To assess students' writing and speaking skills as they complete and present their three-week homework project. This stage allows students to demonstrate their understanding of the topic through a written outcome and a verbal presentation, helping develop both structured writing and confident spoken communication.
Context
Setting Up the Presentations
  • Say: “You’ve worked hard over the past few weeks—following instructions, planning, writing, and improving your work. Now it’s time for the final step: presenting!”
  • Continue: “When you present, remember to speak clearly, make eye contact, and share your ideas with confidence.”
Heads up
Focus
  • Draw Ss attention to the CPT.
Presentation
  • Tell Ss they will complete their projects and present them.
Demonstration
  • Demonstrate the activity with a co-teacher/student.
Heads together
Instruction
  • Use gestures. Say ‘Finish your project’.
  • For those who have finished, have them review their books and complete any unfinished or untidy work.
  • Say: Listen for your name when I call you to present.
ICQs
  • Ask ‘Are we going to keep quiet while others present? (yes)
Presentation and marking
  • Have Ss come to the teacher’s table one by one to present their projects.
  • Mark the written and spoken project using the scoring rubric.
Homework correction
  • Have Ss open to the previous lesson’s homework.
  • Have the Ss pair-share.
  • Review answers, correct mistakes together.

    Stage aims
  • To activate background knowledge
  • To predict the video topic and introduce key vocabulary
  • To prepare students for comprehension
    • Activity 1: Look at the picture. What are they?
    Heads Up
    • Display the picture and ask: “What can you see?”
    • Elicit: “They’re red bags.” or “They’re school bags.”
    • Ask: “What do you think is inside?” to encourage predictions
    • Use gestures or mime to support vocabulary if needed
    Heads Down
    • Students complete the sentence: They are ____________.
    • Monitor and help with spelling or vocabulary as needed
    Heads Together
    • Students compare answers with a partner
    • Elicit responses from the class and confirm correct vocabulary: school bags
    Activity 2: Read the description and complete
    Heads Up
    • Read the short text aloud or have a student read it
    • Ask guiding questions: “Who is packing the bags?” “Where are they?” “Who are the bags for?”
    • Highlight keywords: children, England, school bags
    Heads Down
    • Students read the questions and complete the answers in writing: Who is packing the school bags? / Where are they?
    Heads Together
    • Students compare their answers in pairs
    • Call on a few students to share responses with the class and confirm the answers

    Stage aims
  • To check listening comprehension
  • To introduce and practise “have got / hasn’t got” for talking about possession
  • To link grammar to real-life context from the video
    • Activity 1: Watch the video. Answer the questions
    Heads Up
    • Say: “Now let’s watch the video and find out who the bags are for and what’s inside!”
    • Read the two questions and options aloud
    • Clarify any unfamiliar items (e.g. sharpener, stapler)
    Heads Down
    • Students watch the video and circle their answers individually
    • Monitor and help students focus on listening for key words
    Heads Together
    • Students check answers with a partner
    • Confirm correct answers with the class
    Activity 2: Watch the video again. Answer the questions
    Heads Up
    • Tell students: “Let’s watch again and listen for more details about what’s in the bags”
    • Set the expectation that they will need to remember more items or actions
    Heads Down
    • Students watch a second time and write their answers individually
    • Encourage them to listen for specific school items or actions
    Heads Together
    • Students compare answers with a partner
    • Discuss as a class, confirming details seen in the video
    Activity 3: Learn how to talk about things you have
    Heads Up
    • Show the grammar box and read it aloud
    • Highlight difference between “have got” / “has got” and “haven’t got” / “hasn’t got”
    • Point out examples: “I have got some colouring pencils.” / “I haven’t got colouring pencils.”
    • Ask: “Do you have a glue stick in your pencil case?” and get a few responses
    Heads Down
    • Students read the 4 gap-fill sentences
    • They choose the correct form from the box (have got / hasn’t got / etc.) and write answers
    • Monitor and help with accuracy
    Heads Together
    • Students compare their completed sentences with a partner
    • Call on volunteers to read the answers aloud
    • Confirm the correct answers with the class
    Reward moment
  • Centre yourself, settle the class.
  • Check behaviour expectations.
  • Reward/remind and award points.

    Stage aims
  • To check understanding of video content
  • To develop empathy and critical thinking
  • To encourage personal responses and cultural connections
    • Activity 1: Match to complete the sentences
    Heads Up
    • Read the three sentence stems aloud: “They put…”, “They walk to get money…”, “They have got…”
    • Ask students to look at the options a–c and read them together
    • Clarify the meaning of key phrases (e.g. “to buy school bags”)
    Heads Down
    • Students match 1–3 with a–c to complete the sentences
    • Monitor and assist with comprehension and accuracy
    Heads Together
    • Students compare answers with a partner
    • Elicit correct answers from the class and confirm
    Activity 2: Partner discussion
    Heads Up
    • Read the three discussion questions aloud
    • Model a response using the example: “I feel sad. I want to help…”
    • Encourage emotional language and use of target structures like want to
    Heads Down
    • Students think about their answers and write 1–2 sentences individually
    • Support students with vocabulary or sentence starters if needed
    Heads Together
    • Students discuss their answers with a partner
    • Ask for volunteers to share their responses with the class
    Culture Comparison
    Heads Up
    • Say: “Now let’s think about children in Vietnam who can’t go to school”
    • Read the questions aloud and check understanding
    • Model the example sentence using the board if needed
    Heads Down
    • Students write a few sentences using the prompt to describe the situation and how they feel
    Heads Together
    • Discuss in small groups or pairs
    • Invite groups or individuals to share their ideas with the class
    Reward moment
  • Centre yourself, settle the class.
  • Check behaviour expectations.
  • Reward/remind and award points.
