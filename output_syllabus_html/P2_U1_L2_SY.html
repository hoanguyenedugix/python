Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six toys (ball, bat, football, skateboard, scooter, yo-yo) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show toy flashcards. Say the word. Ask: “Where’s the scooter?” Students point in SB.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Toy Detective❞ – Describe a classmate with toy vocabulary: “He’s got a skateboard.” Students guess who it is. Then, students take turns describing classmates using “has got” sentences.

Students can say simple statements about possessions using "He's/She's got a.../He/She hasn't got a..." and ask and answer simple questions using "Has he/she got a...?" with responses like "Yes, he/she has." / "No, he/she hasn't."

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Show a picture of a child. Ask: “Has he got a yo-yo?” Students answer: “Yes, he has.” or “No, he hasn’t.”

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Toy Detective❞ – Describe a classmate with toy vocabulary: “He’s got a skateboard.” Students guess who it is. Then, students take turns describing classmates using “has got” sentences.

Students can say statements using the target structure “He's/She's got a ___.” / “He/She hasn't got a ___.” and ask and answer questions using the target structure "Has he/she got a ___?" / "Yes, he/she has." / "No, he/she hasn't." to talk about possessions.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write 3 example pairs on the board: “She’s got a ball.” / “He hasn’t got a scooter.” Students make their own using SB visuals.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Toy Detective❞ – Describe a classmate with toy vocabulary: “He’s got a skateboard.” Students guess who it is. Then, students take turns describing classmates using “has got” sentences.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Food:

  • Food in syllabi typically Western-centric;
  • banh mi >> bread

2. Holidays and travel:

  • Certain common holiday activities are less likely to overlap with those of Western kids (eating seafood at the beach, going on tours in large groups)

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
