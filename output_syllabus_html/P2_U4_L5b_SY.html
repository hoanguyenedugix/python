Lesson Outcomes

Students can correctly use the verb “to be” in singular and plural, positive and negative sentences.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write a sentence scramble: “is / a / She / nurse.” Students unscramble, write and read out. Repeat for “They are firefighters.”

Stretch Strategies:

  • Spot the Mistake Detective – Give stronger students incorrect job sentences (e.g., He are a firefighter. or They is teachers.) to correct and explain the error to their group.

Students can recognise and identify the sounds “ng” and “ck”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Phonics) Say “kick,” “sing,” “wing,” “sock.” Students repeat and raise one hand for “ng” sound, two hands for “ck.”

Stretch Strategies:

  • Spot the Mistake Detective – Give stronger students incorrect job sentences (e.g., He are a firefighter. or They is teachers.) to correct and explain the error to their group.

Students can complete short sentence frames using the correct form of the verb “to be”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Writing) Write: “He ___ a teacher.” Students complete with “is/isn’t.” Extend to “They ___ students.” for plural practice.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Spot the Mistake Detective – Give stronger students incorrect job sentences (e.g., He are a firefighter. or They is teachers.) to correct and explain the error to their group.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

2. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.
