Lesson Outcomes

Students can correctly use “have/has got” in positive and negative statements and Yes/No questions.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) On the board write: “I’ve got a ___ / I haven’t got a ___.” and “He’s got a ___ / He hasn’t got a ___.” Students complete orally with toys.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Sentence Build Relay❞ – Divide the class into two teams. On the board, write “He/She has/hasn’t got ___.” Students take turns running to complete the sentence using toy flashcards or pictures, then read it aloud.

Students can recognise and identify the sounds “th” and “ee”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Phonics) Write th, ee on the board. Say: “/th/ is in ‘three’. /ee/ is in ‘feet.’” Students find more words in the SB.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Sentence Build Relay❞ – Divide the class into two teams. On the board, write “He/She has/hasn’t got ___.” Students take turns running to complete the sentence using toy flashcards or pictures, then read it aloud.

Students can complete short sentence frames to answer Yes/No questions about possessions.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Food:

  • Food in syllabi typically Western-centric;
  • banh mi >> bread

2. Holidays and travel:

  • Certain common holiday activities are less likely to overlap with those of Western kids (eating seafood at the beach, going on tours in large groups)

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
