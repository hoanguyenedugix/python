Lesson Outcomes

Students can correctly ask and answer “What time...?” questions (e.g., “What time does he get up?” / “He gets up at seven thirty.”).

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write scrambled question: “do / What / you / time / get up?” Students unscramble and answer with: “I get up at ___.”

Stretch Strategies:

  • Students create a “Guess the person” quiz using daily routines: “This person gets up at 6:30. He has lunch at 11:45. Who is it?” Classmates ask follow-up “What time...?” questions to guess the classmate.

Students can recognise and identify the vowel sounds “ai” and “oa”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Phonics) Play sound sort: /ai/ or /oa/? Say words (e.g., “train,” “goat”), students raise hands to signal sound group and copy in SB.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Students create a “Guess the person” quiz using daily routines: “This person gets up at 6:30. He has lunch at 11:45. Who is it?” Classmates ask follow-up “What time...?” questions to guess the classmate.

Students can complete short sentence frames (e.g., “What time do you get up?”) using the correct question words and verb forms.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Writing) Use sentence frame: “What time ___ you go to bed?” Students complete the question and answer it in writing using correct verb form.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Students create a “Guess the person” quiz using daily routines: “This person gets up at 6:30. He has lunch at 11:45. Who is it?” Classmates ask follow-up “What time...?” questions to guess the classmate.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

2. Hobbies and activities:

  • A wide variety of syllabus hobbies may be less popular or available to some Vietnamese students (practising the flute; seeing famous paintings), although many overlap

3. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

4. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.
