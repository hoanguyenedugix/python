Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six beach objects (rubber ring, water gun, boat, beach ball, life jacket, bucket) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show flashcards of beach objects. Say each word. Students repeat and then match them to SB images.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Yes/No Beach Sort❞ – Place flashcards (or drawings) of beach items on the board. Say sentences like “I’ve got a boat” or “I haven’t got a beach ball.” Students come up and move the flashcard to the Yes or No side. Then, they take turns saying their own sentences for a partner to sort.

Students can say simple statements about possessions using "I've got a.../I haven't got a..." and ask and answer simple questions using "Have you got a...?" with responses like "Yes, I have." / "No, I haven't."

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Use classroom items or cards. Ask: “Have you got a rubber ring?” Students respond using “Yes, I have” or “No, I haven’t.”

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Yes/No Beach Sort❞ – Place flashcards (or drawings) of beach items on the board. Say sentences like “I’ve got a boat” or “I haven’t got a beach ball.” Students come up and move the flashcard to the Yes or No side. Then, they take turns saying their own sentences for a partner to sort.

Students can say statements using the target structure “I've got a ___.” / “I haven't got a ___.” and ask and answer questions using the target structure "Have you got a ___?" / "Yes, I have." / "No, I haven't." to talk about possessions.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write the question and answer on the board: “Have you got a ___?” / “Yes, I have.” Students practise with a partner using objects from SB.

Stretch Strategies:

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Food:

  • Food in syllabi typically Western-centric;
  • banh mi >> bread

2. Holidays and travel:

  • Certain common holiday activities are less likely to overlap with those of Western kids (eating seafood at the beach, going on tours in large groups)

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
