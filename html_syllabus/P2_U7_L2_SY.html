Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six more actions (dance, sing, jump, walk, laugh, run) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show pictures of actions (run, sing, jump). Say each one. Students repeat and group by “fast/slow” or “quiet/loud” to deepen understanding.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Mime Detective: One student silently mimes an action (e.g., jumping, dancing), and others must ask yes/no questions until they guess correctly. Encourage full answers: “Yes, he is.” / “No, he isn’t.”

Students can ask and answer simple Yes/No questions using “Are you...?” / “Is he/she...?” and respond with “Yes, I am.” / “No, I’m not.” or “Yes, he/she is.” / “No, he/she isn’t.”

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Model: “Are you jumping?” Mime the action. Students respond “Yes, I am.” or “No, I’m not.” Repeat with peer Q&A using action flashcards.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Mime Detective: One student silently mimes an action (e.g., jumping, dancing), and others must ask yes/no questions until they guess correctly. Encourage full answers: “Yes, he is.” / “No, he isn’t.”

Students can use the target structure “Are you ___?” / “Yes, I am.” / “No, I’m not.” and “Is he/she ___?” / “Yes, he/she is.” / “No, he/she isn’t.” to talk about actions.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Display sentence frames: “Is he ___?” / “Yes, he is.” Drill with class using flashcards, then practise in pairs.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Mime Detective: One student silently mimes an action (e.g., jumping, dancing), and others must ask yes/no questions until they guess correctly. Encourage full answers: “Yes, he is.” / “No, he isn’t.”

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Present continuous form:

  • The -ing is often forgotten. Vietnamese does not have different forms of the same verb. I like eat rice.

2. Silent letters:

  • Systematic kinds of silent letters, such as 'magic e' in late or mote, or silent gh in thought and bought, are entirely alien to Vietnamese. They are often acquired as sight words first; the teacher can explain at higher levels.

3. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

5. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.
