Lesson Outcomes

Students can recognise and name six sports (football, tennis, basketball, badminton, volleyball, table tennis) when listening and looking at visual prompts.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Vocab) Show sport flashcards or images from SB. Ask: “What’s this?” – “It’s volleyball.” Students repeat and sort into “ball” vs. “racket” sports.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Activity Survey
  • Students conduct a class survey using the target question: “What do you do after school?” Then, they write two or three sentences summarizing the data using third-person forms (e.g., “Ben plays tennis. Anna plays volleyball.”).

Students can ask and answer simple questions about sports using “What do you do after school?” / “What does he/she do after school?” and respond with “I play...” / “He/She plays...”.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Ask: “What do you do after school?” Model: “I play basketball.” Students interview partners and report back: “He plays…”

Stretch Strategies:

  • Activity Survey
  • Students conduct a class survey using the target question: “What do you do after school?” Then, they write two or three sentences summarizing the data using third-person forms (e.g., “Ben plays tennis. Anna plays volleyball.”).

Students can use the target structure “What do you do after school?” / “I play ___.” and “What does he/she do after school?” / “He/She plays ___.” to talk about sports.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Grammar) Write and drill: “What does she do after school?” – “She plays badminton.” Students practise with partner swap activity using picture cues.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Activity Survey
  • Students conduct a class survey using the target question: “What do you do after school?” Then, they write two or three sentences summarizing the data using third-person forms (e.g., “Ben plays tennis. Anna plays volleyball.”).

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Question formation with do-support:

  • Bạn có thích ăn bánh mì không? >> you like eat bread? [do you like to eat bánh mì?]

2. Negatives with do-support:

  • Con không thích >> I no/not like [I don't like]

3. Sport and play:

  • Playground words tend to be long and phonetically difficult (slide, see-saw, swings, climbing frame)
  • Some sports not well known in VN (baseball, cricket, rugby, paragliding, water skiing)

4. Hobbies and activities:

  • A wide variety of syllabus hobbies may be less popular or available to some Vietnamese students (practising the flute; seeing famous paintings), although many overlap

5. Unstressed vowels (schwa):

  • Unstressed vowels do not really exist in Vietnamese, so these need extra practice. Make sure the final vowel of computer is a light schwa, not a heavy com-pu-TER. Same with the final vowel of banana. Or the second syllable of elephant. And so on.

6. Inversion in questions:

  • Bạn muốn ăn gì? >> you want to eat what? [what do you want to eat?]
  • This is extremely difficult to master, especially combined with do-support.
