Lesson Outcomes

Students can listen to a story and identify key characters and toys mentioned.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Listening) Before the story, preview toys in the SB. Say: “Listen for skateboard, football…” Students raise hands when they hear the word.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Change-the-Story❞ – After acting out the story, ask students to imagine a new toy or location. They say or draw how the story would change (e.g., “He’s got a scooter at the zoo!”).

Students can read the story aloud, act it out, and answer questions using the target language.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Speaking) Read the story aloud and pause after each sentence. Students echo with gestures. Assign roles for pair acting.

Stretch Strategies:

  • ❝Change-the-Story❞ – After acting out the story, ask students to imagine a new toy or location. They say or draw how the story would change (e.g., “He’s got a scooter at the zoo!”).

Students can create a simple, imaginative ending to the story by drawing or acting it out.

Future Skills

Support Strategies:

  • (4Cs – Creativity) Ask: “What happens after the story?” Students draw or mime a new ending using known characters.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Food:

  • Food in syllabi typically Western-centric;
  • banh mi >> bread

2. Holidays and travel:

  • Certain common holiday activities are less likely to overlap with those of Western kids (eating seafood at the beach, going on tours in large groups)

3. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.

4. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.
