Lesson Outcomes

Students can listen and identify key details about the daily routine of some children in Australia by writing the correct times.

Communication

Support Strategies:

  • (Listening) Play video in chunks. Ask: “What time do the children have lunch?” Students write the correct time or match from options.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Students create a Venn diagram comparing their daily routine to the child in the video (e.g., “We both eat dinner at 7:00,” “I go to school earlier”). Then, they present 2–3 differences using full sentences.

Students can say how their daily routine is similar to or different from the children in the video.

Real world learning

Support Strategies:

  • (Social Studies) Draw a 2-column chart: “Me / Kids in Australia.” Compare routines: bedtime, meals, activities.

Stretch Strategies:

  • Students create a Venn diagram comparing their daily routine to the child in the video (e.g., “We both eat dinner at 7:00,” “I go to school earlier”). Then, they present 2–3 differences using full sentences.

Students can compare their own life to life on a farm in Australia and explain why people have different routines.

Future Skills

Support Strategies:

  • (4Cs – Critical Thinking) Ask: “Why do the boys wake up early?” “How is life on a farm different?” Prompt students to reflect and share.

Language Transfer Analysis

1. Consonant clusters:

  • Consonant clusters do not exist in Vietnamese words, so they need special attention. Remember that clusters don't come only at the beginning of words – end clusters can be even harder (dogs, task, crisp, crisps, snacks)

2. Hobbies and activities:

  • A wide variety of syllabus hobbies may be less popular or available to some Vietnamese students (practising the flute; seeing famous paintings), although many overlap

3. Released final consonants:

  • In Vietnamese, final consonants are 'held in' – you don't actually hear them released. But in English, the final consonant of cat, top, rock, and so on are released with a puff of air. Make sure you are aware of this difference.

4. Contractions:

  • Do not exist in standard Vietnamese, written or spoken. The hardest part for YLs is learning to pronounce them naturally: there's a dog rather than there is a dog.
